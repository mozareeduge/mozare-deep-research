# Source routing, roles, and selection

## Source lanes

- **Academic Tools:** identifiers, abstracts, citation/reference chains, scholarly metadata, and accessible paper sections.
- **WebSearch/WebFetch:** publishers, author pages, primary web texts, archives, institutional pages, repositories, syllabi, standards, and current public material.
- **Zotero:** the user's library, annotations, metadata, and bounded pages when relevant and not excluded.
- **Direct local files:** user-provided books, articles, archives, code, images, datasets, or notes.

A lane is a route to a source, not a quality score.

## Source roles

Select sources because they fill an evidential function:

- `primary-core`: explicitly names, defines, consolidates, or centrally performs the concept;
- `primary-adjacent`: precursor, neighboring practice, or related primary formulation;
- `scholarly-interpretation`: sustained academic analysis;
- `critique`: credible challenge, counterposition, or limitation;
- `reception`: later uptake, pedagogy, institutionalization, or transformation;
- `context`: historical or technical background;
- `discovery-only`: leads to stronger sources but is not used as evidence.

## Search strategies

Use distinct strategies before repeating variants:

1. exact term and spelling variants;
2. named authors and canonical works;
3. official, publisher, archive, and institutional primary pages;
4. backward and forward citation chains;
5. precursor and adjacent concepts;
6. criticism, controversy, and counterposition;
7. later reception and pedagogy;
8. non-English searches when substantively relevant.

Record exact queries, providers, result counts where available, and selected source IDs in `queries.jsonl`.

## Access ladder

Move through the smallest sufficient access level:

`metadata -> abstract/description -> bounded section/pages -> close reading`

Do not use metadata or a search snippet for a content claim. Do not silently replace an inaccessible central primary source with a secondary paraphrase. Mark `preview_only`, `paywalled`, or `unavailable` and label indirect evidence.

## Selection quality

For central claims prefer direct primary materials, original research, credible academic interpretation, and authoritative records. Evaluate source fit across directness, genre, edition/version, access, relevance, methodological transparency, independence, and historical position. Citation count is only a discovery signal.

Avoid source monocultures created by one index, one bibliography, one institution, or one repeated citation chain. Books, chapters, archives, and non-indexed humanities materials may be more important than easily ranked papers.
